More than 91,000 D.C. residents at High Risk of Losing Medicaid

By Sinyi Au

More than 91,000 low-income residents in the nation's capital are at high risk of losing their health insurance under the sweeping tax and spending Republic bill that imposes the most significant changes to the Medicaid program in a generation.

The at-risk group – primarily working adults covered by the Affordable Care Act expansion – represents one-third of the 272,249 people in the District of Columbia who relied on the public insurance program as of June 2025, according to a data analysis of the legislation and enrollment figures from the D.C. Department of Health Care Finance.

The bill fundamentally re-engineers the health safety net by mandating strict work requirements and slashing the federal funding mechanisms that D.C. and other state depend on. The result is a looming health crisis that will disproportionately affect the city’s most vulnerable.

“These cuts are the inevitable result of federal decisions in this legislation, which is shifting costs to states and in turn forcing them to make really painful, harsh cuts that will take away coverage and health services from low income individuals and families,” says Edwin Park, a Research Professor at Georgetown University's McCourt School of Public Policy.

A Wall of Paperwork

The primary mechanism that puts over 91,000 lives in jeopardy is a new “community engagement” mandate. Starting in 2027, most adults covered under the ACA expansion must document 80 hours per month of work, training or community service to stay insured.

For those who lose coverage, there is no safety net. A KFF analysis highlights a provision that creates a “coverage lock-out,” explicitly barring anyone disenrolled due to the work requirements from receiving subsidized health insurance on the ACA Marketplace. They will be pushed directly into the ranks of the uninsured.

Squeezing the system from both ends

While residents face new barriers to care, the District’s entire healthcare system is being squeezed financially. The law simultaneously reduces funding for the city and increases the strain on its limited resources.

“When Medicaid is cut, fewer people are enrolled and more uninsured, that puts pressures on the District’s relatively limited healthcare system,” Park says, noting that the city has just “a handful of hospitals” to absorb the impact of a newly uninsured population seeking more expensive emergency care.

This pressure is applied to a system already in a financially precarious position, unique to the District.

“Congress has restricted the ability of the District to spend money that it has already raised through taxes,” Park says. “The District is already facing a shortfall, even though it has the funding to make that spending.”

The new law intensifies this crisis by systematically dismantling the financial architecture of the D.C. Medicaid program. It phases down the city’s ability to use provider taxes, a key tool for drawing down federal matching funds.

It also caps supplemental payments to the safety-net hospitals that serve the highest volumes of Medicaid patients and eliminates the enhanced federal match for providing emergency care to certain immigrants.
